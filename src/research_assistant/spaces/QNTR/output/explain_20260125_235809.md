## 1. Conceptual Definition
**Digital Resource Orchestration (DRO)** extends **resource orchestration** to incorporate an organization's **digital infrastructure**—defined as institutions, practices, and protocols that organize and deliver digital technology's power to business and society—alongside traditional resources, technology, and digital services.[3] It represents leaders' actions to structure the resource portfolio, bundle resources into capabilities, and leverage them organization-wide to realize competitive advantage, serving as a key element of **IT governance** and **digital transformation**, as illustrated in healthcare cases like Västra Götaland.[3]

## 2. Theoretical Foundation
DRO is grounded in **resource orchestration theory (RO)**, which integrates the **Resource-Based View (RBV)** and **dynamic capabilities** frameworks.[3][6][7] RBV emphasizes that resources (assets, capabilities, processes, knowledge) under organizational control must be strategically managed to generate value, while dynamic capabilities highlight managerial actions for adaptation; RO synthesizes these into **structuring**, **bundling**, and **leveraging** processes.[3][6][7] This mirrors the course's **research philosophies** and **research process**, where orchestrated actions ensure resources yield valid outcomes, akin to hypothesis testing and data visualization for **measures of central tendency and dispersion**.[KB1][3]

## 3. Key Components
From the **resource orchestration framework**, DRO's main elements, adapted for digital contexts, are:[3][7]
- **Structuring the resource portfolio**: Leaders identify, acquire, and organize digital assets (e.g., cloud systems, IT protocols) with traditional resources via mechanisms like centralized procurement.[3]
- **Bundling resources into capabilities**: Combining digital infrastructure, technology, and services to form cohesive capabilities that abstract system complexities.[3][7]
- **Leveraging capabilities**: Deploying bundled resources across the organization to achieve outcomes like competitive advantage or digital transformation goals.[3][6]

## 4. Practical Application
In practice, DRO streamlines end-to-end processes by unifying IT services, resources, and digital ecosystems, as in healthcare where it coordinates digital infrastructure for service delivery.[3][1] For instance, orchestration platforms automate workflows across cloud and on-premises environments, enabling real-time monitoring, resource provisioning/deprovisioning, and ML-driven optimization—reducing manual scripting and enhancing efficiency in research data pipelines using **SPSS, R, or JASP**.[1][2][KB1] In digital transformation, it matches **strategic logic** with resource actions, as traditional intermediaries evolve by coordinating digital assets for goal achievement.[4]

## 5. Research Considerations
Researchers should approach DRO through quantitative lenses like the course's **research process**: formulate hypotheses on structuring/bundling/leveraging effects, design measurements for digital constructs (e.g., scales for capability efficacy), sample from real-world cases (e.g., healthcare[3]), and analyze via **SPSS/AMOS/R** for validity checks and visualization.[KB1][3][6] Test RBV/dynamic capabilities integration empirically, controlling for **strategic logic**[4], and ensure **internal/external validity** via hands-on projects with real-life data—addressing gaps like ML optimization by extending RO frameworks.[1][6][7]
